One of the beautiful eye-opening insights I gained from studying Arabic poetry literature is the reason why the "Hamasah" genre emerged. Before stating it, however, a historical prologue will go a long way.

# The Hamasah genre

In the first third of the 9th century ~= 220 AH (835 CE), the renowned poet Abu Tammam, considered by some scholars to be one of the three pillars of Arabic poetry (alongside Al-Buhturi and Al-Mutanabbi), was blocked in [Hamadan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamadan) by a winter storm and decided to write a special type of book: a collection of short parts from famous or unsung poems. He named it after its first chapter ("Al-Hamasah"), roughly translated to valour, which was the tradition of those times, naming books by their first chapter's name. This was the same practice as with the [Kalīla wa-Dimna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kal%C4%ABla_wa-Dimna) fables collection. It is worth mentioning that the editors of the [Hamasah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamasah) Wikipedia page missed this nuance when writing about this genre:

![image](/assets/img/hamasah_assets/image.png)

It doesn't only contain chivalrous odes; it also has love passages, verses discussing good manners, and others more.

Following Abu Tammam, famous poets and scholars mimicked Habib and created their own versions of it, namely Hamasat Al-Buhturi (< 284 AH), Hamasat al-zurafa (Poems of the Refined and Witty) by Al-Abdalakani al-Zawzani, and [many more](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamasah):

![image](/assets/img/hamasah_assets/image%201.png)

One important thing to note is that this is not the first work of literature where a collection of poetry was assembled together (albeit very close to the start of the written form tradition, which was in the 7th century CE due to the [revelation of the Quran](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabic_literature#:~:text=Arabic%20literature%2C%20primarily,%5B3%5D)). There were actually many books written in that sense, for example: [Mufaddaliyyat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mufaddaliyat) ~= 145-168 AH (762-784 CE) by [Al-Mufaddal ad-Dabbi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al-Mufaddal_ad-Dabbi), [Asmaiyyat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asma%27iyyat) by [Al-Asmai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al-Asma%CA%BFi) 122–213/218 AH (740–828/833 CE), Jamharat Ash'ar al-Arab by Abu Zayd al-Qurashi, who died at 170 AH (786 CE), and many more. It is worth mentioning that the [Islamic Golden Age](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islamic_Golden_Age) is traditionally understood to have begun during the reign of the Abbasid caliph Harun al-Rashid, 169 AH – 193 AH (786–809 CE). That is when the urbanization of Islamic society reached its highest rate of growth. Let's plot these numbers on a line and see if we notice a pattern:

![image](/assets/img/hamasah_assets/image%202.png)

Can you notice something?

What you could've noticed is that the hamasah genre showed coincidentally or not alongside the Islamic civilization spike. Can that be a fluke, or is there a theorem that can explain such an emergence of short-form content?

# Social Acceleration

Welcome to the "[Social acceleration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_acceleration)" phenomenon, a sociological concept pioneered by [Hartmut Rosa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hartmut_Rosa). It describes how, with recent technological advancement and globalisation of our society, the pace that our ancestors used to live with has become insufficient to keep up with such changes. A linear time that manifested in recurring events like seasons or day-night cycles has become insufficient, and an abstract time has emerged with a different slope depending on the necessity of the person and their environment — mostly sharper, as our vibrant society becomes more radioactive.

In the same framework of Rosa, I would like to append (perhaps Rosa has suggested it, but I haven't read his writings extensively) that this social acceleration phenomenon is a recurring event with each empire's rise — think of Ibn Khaldun's [four stages of empire](https://www.philosopheasy.com/p/ibn-khalduns-four-stages-of-empire) theory. This fits nicely with my proposition: the hidden reason behind the appearance of the "Hamasah" genre is the social acceleration brought about after the civilization explosion the Islamic region encountered in the late 7th century CE. The Hamasah genre was a [shorter form of entertainment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_acceleration#:~:text=Additionally%2C%20social%20acceleration%20is%20evident%20on%20streaming%20services%20and%20social%20media%20trends%2C%20intensifying%20the%20enormous%20pressure%20to%20stay%20current%20with%20ever%2Devolving%20content%20and%20interactions.) that the nature of that abstract time required. With more sophisticated societies come faster forms of entertainment consumption — in our time, before it was books, then journals, then cinema and films; now it is reels, shorts, and TikToks, everywhere we look.
